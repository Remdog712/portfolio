A Glass Slipper or A Golden Slipper?

An analytical breakdown of the word usage between Disney's Cinderella and The Grimm Brothers: Ash Maiden


Disney's: Cinderella and The Grimm Brothers: Ash Maiden are fairy tales. Made from the same concepts, telling the same stories but with different choices of words.
Hence why many people know Cinderella but have no clue of the Ash Maiden.
HINT They're one in the same

The Name

We can see that "Cinderella" and "(ash)Maiden" are the most prominent words
The definition of "cinder" is "a small piece of partly burned coal or wood that has stopped giving off flames but still has combustible matter in it." and "ella" in certain languages denotes "feminine," so Cinder Ella would be the equivalent to Ash Maiden




4-Grams

Notice that "oh sweet nightingale" and "the king's son" are the most frequent 4-gram. Both can be roughly viewed as a prince or someone who will save the maiden in cliche fairytales.




The Slipper

Both stories use slippers but Cinderella uses a Glass Slipper while Ash Maiden uses a Golden Slipper to identify the "fair lady"




The Terms

We can see that "Oh" is the most prominent word in Cinderella with 104 uses. The context being that it's used as an almost joyous expression. Something you'd expect from a children's movie/story and as well with "singing" at 34 uses, but Ash Maiden doesn't have many of or if any of these joyous expression words. Simply telling a story without overly excited expressions.




The Conclusion

Both stories tell the same story that many know and love, but their use of words is what seperates them. One being a beloved children's story while another tells a more cold feeling story; Bland words and all.
